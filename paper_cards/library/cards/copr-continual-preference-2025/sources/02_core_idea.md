COPR represents prior learning by historical optimal policies rather than by replaying a fixed raw-data pool alone. It derives a moderate reward from the pairwise ranking loss so that new training does not endlessly sharpen a preference gap. The resulting policy distributions become constraints on the next update, connecting the current task to a record of earlier optimization decisions.

This produces a compromise between new and old preference evidence. A Lagrangian-dual formulation balances the objective for the incoming task against the policy-regularization constraints, while the moderate reward is intended to preserve generation diversity. The important data-use change is that each new batch is evaluated in relation to the historical policies that previous batches produced.
