For a sequence of preference tasks, COPR takes the current task's prompt-response comparisons and the historical optimal policies learned on preceding tasks. It converts the pairwise ranking signal into a moderate reward, parameterizes the sampling distributions associated with current and historical policies, and uses those distributions to form new learning objectives and constraints. A dual optimization procedure then updates the policy while enforcing the historical conditions.

The paper evaluates task-incremental and domain-incremental continual alignment. It compares COPR with continual-learning and iterative-alignment baselines using reward-based measures, GPT-4 evaluation, human assessment, and generation-diversity statistics. Reproduction needs the task order, task boundaries, preference-data versions, prior checkpoints or policy distributions, constraint weights, evaluation prompts, and the exact policy-update schedule; changing the order changes the learning problem.
