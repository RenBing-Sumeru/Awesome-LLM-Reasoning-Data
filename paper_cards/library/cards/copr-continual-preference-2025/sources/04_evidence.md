The paper reports that COPR outperforms its compared continual-learning baselines in the proposed continual-alignment benchmark under reward-based metrics, GPT-4 evaluation, and human assessment. Its diversity analysis is central to the claim: the authors measure generation diversity alongside task performance because a method could preserve a reward score by collapsing to a narrow response style. The paper also supplies theoretical learnability analysis for the constrained formulation.

The evidence supports a narrower conclusion than universal continual alignment: under the reported task and domain sequences, historical-policy constraints can improve the balance of new-task adaptation, retention, and diversity relative to the baselines. It does not establish that policy distributions are sufficient summaries of all earlier human preferences, or that the same constraints resolve direct conflicts between old and new values. Those boundaries should remain visible when adapting the method.
