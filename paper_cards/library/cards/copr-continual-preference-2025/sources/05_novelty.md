Many continual-learning approaches preserve parameters, replay examples, or regularize outputs, while ordinary offline preference optimization treats the newest pairwise data as the main object. COPR changes the optimization record by promoting historical optimal-policy distributions to explicit constraints. The moderate reward also targets a failure mode specific to repeated preference optimization: excessive widening of the preferred-dispreferred gap and the resulting preference collapse.

The novelty is not that historical data matters, nor that constrained optimization and policy regularization exist. The reusable change is to derive a policy-level representation of earlier preference learning and use it as a formal condition for consuming the next batch. Before reuse, check how prior policies are stored or approximated, whether the constraints preserve harmful legacy behavior, and whether a benchmark's task order reflects the intended deployment stream.
