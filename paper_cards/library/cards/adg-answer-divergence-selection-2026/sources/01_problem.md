A fixed supervised fine-tuning budget cannot absorb every available instruction, yet common selectors often infer usefulness from one reference response, one loss value, or one uncertainty estimate. A single answer can confuse harmless stylistic variation with a genuine learning gap, while a high-loss prompt may simply be ambiguous or poorly specified. The result is that a small training set can spend capacity on redundant examples or on disagreement that offers no teachable structure.

ADG asks whether several answers sampled from the intended backbone reveal a richer selection signal than one target alone. It describes each instruction by the geometry of five generated answers, then selects 10K instruction records from public pools for SFT. The question is not whether the answers are already correct, but whether their pattern of variation indicates an example whose consumption may improve the model under a fixed training budget.
