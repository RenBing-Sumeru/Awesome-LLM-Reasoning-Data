Large instruction corpora such as Alpaca contain useful examples alongside redundant, noisy, or poorly matched records. Selecting examples through a general-purpose judge can import that judge's stylistic biases, while ordinary influence methods estimate only each record's isolated contribution. An additive score cannot express that two individually useful records may repeat the same information or jointly harm a target behavior.

JI2S asks which records from an instruction pool should be admitted to a very small SFT subset when the desired behavior is represented by high-quality human preference examples. The training decision is the ranking and selection of instruction-response pairs, not an inference-time technique. It seeks a model-dependent subset whose records have favorable individual influence and favorable interactions with other records relative to a proxy evaluation set.
