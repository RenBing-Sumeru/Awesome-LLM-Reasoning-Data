MMPO treats a chosen and rejected answer as two samples used to approximate the marginal likelihood of generating the desired answer from an input. Its numerically stable log-sum-exp objective yields gradients that raise the chosen answer's score more than the rejected answer's score, with the amount determined by their current score difference. Preference behavior emerges from that weighting rather than from a separately learned reward model.

The implementation normalizes chosen and rejected scores within a batch and retains a DPO-style auxiliary term. The authors argue that normalization and the subtraction inside log-sum-exp stop scores from growing without bound, making beta less brittle. Thus the method does not filter records; it changes the optimization geometry through which every pair is consumed.
