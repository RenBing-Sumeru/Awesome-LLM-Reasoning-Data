Offline preference alignment normally receives a fixed set of chosen-rejected pairs, but the objective used to consume those pairs can be highly sensitive to its regularization scale. DPO simplifies RLHF yet retains a contrastive objective derived from reward maximization, while related objectives can either drift or lose general capability as the hyperparameter changes. This makes a static preference dataset behave differently under small changes in optimization settings.

MMPO asks whether preferred responses can be learned from the same pairs through a simpler likelihood principle rather than an explicit reward formulation or an entropy bonus. It targets the objective that turns records into gradients. The paper is therefore about the training use of existing pairwise labels, not about collecting more labels or generating better candidates.
