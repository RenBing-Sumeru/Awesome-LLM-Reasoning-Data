Most direct preference objectives start from a contrastive surrogate of reward maximization. MMPO instead starts from the marginal likelihood of a desired output and uses the pair as a two-sample approximation. The key novelty is not the use of a different dataset but a derivation showing that this likelihood objective implicitly carries out preference optimization through data-dependent chosen-versus-rejected gradient weights.

The method combines this reformulation with batch normalization and a retained DPO-style auxiliary term, then tests which pieces trade preference win rate against general capability. This makes the contribution more than a new algebraic loss: it offers a hypothesis about why an offline preference objective can be less sensitive to beta while consuming identical labeled records.
