Preference learning is usually described through its loss, while the structure of the chosen-rejected records is left to curation heuristics. It is therefore unclear whether common choices such as giving two answers a shared prefix, maximizing their score gap, or training on very difficult prompts actually improve learning. Without controlled evidence, a corpus may preserve expensive structure that has little value or discard low-contrast cases that are useful in combination.

This paper asks which attributes of preference records change downstream instruction-following performance. It creates a controlled setting where responses can be scored by code against verifiable constraints, then varies pair construction while holding data size fixed. The aim is to identify how a record should be constructed for training, rather than to introduce another preference-optimization objective.
