The paper turns preference-data curation into a factorial experiment. It synthesizes instruction-following prompts containing four, five, or six combinations from 23 code-verifiable constraints, then generates and scores responses. Rejection sampling creates independently generated chosen-rejected pairs, whereas Monte Carlo Tree Search creates pairs that share a prefix and diverge later; their structure can therefore be compared without changing the evaluation contract.

It examines three dimensions: common prefixes, the contrast and absolute scores of the two responses, and prompt difficulty. The central idea is not that one universal pair type wins. High contrast is beneficial when used alone, a mixture of high- and low-contrast pairs can be better, shared prefixes provide small but stable gains, and moderate prompt difficulty produces better generalization than excessive difficulty in the reported setting.
