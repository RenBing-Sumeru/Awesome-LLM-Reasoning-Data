Across controlled configurations, MCTS-generated pairs with shared prefixes consistently but only marginally outperform rejection-sampled pairs; the paper therefore does not claim that search complexity is always justified. It also finds that high-contrast pairs outperform low-contrast pairs when used alone, while combinations of high and low contrast sometimes perform best. The results distinguish the contrast between chosen and rejected answers from their absolute correctness, with the contrast generally carrying the larger effect.

For difficulty, models trained on moderate four-constraint prompts outperform models trained on very difficult six-constraint prompts, including on six-constraint evaluations. The paper attributes part of this to low preference-pair yield and lower learning efficiency for overly difficult prompts. These findings are supported by fixed-size comparisons across rejection sampling and MCTS, but are bounded to code-verifiable instruction following and the reported 8B-scale models rather than subjective alignment settings.
