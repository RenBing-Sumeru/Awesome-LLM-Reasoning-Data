Offline preference optimization usually gives every chosen-rejected pair the same target form, even though some labels are decisive and others are nearly tied. A small reward margin can reflect ambiguity or noise, but a fixed-margin objective can push the model to fit it as confidently as a clear safety or helpfulness contrast. Filtering these pairs removes data but also discards potentially useful evidence.

γ-PO asks whether the confidence signaled by a pair's reward margin should change how that record enters the optimization objective. It studies existing preference pairs rather than constructing a new corpus. The central decision is to make the target margin instance-specific, so high-confidence comparisons exert a sharper training signal while ambiguous comparisons are softened instead of treated as equally reliable.
