Chosen and rejected answers often share boilerplate, repeated facts, or semantically close wording. Ordinary DPO raises the probability of a token on the chosen path and lowers it on the rejected path even when the two occurrences express nearly the same content. The paper argues that these opposing token-level updates can cancel or confuse the contrastive signal instead of concentrating learning on the part that explains the preference.

AAO asks how an existing preference pair can separate semantically redundant background from content that actually distinguishes the two answers. It leaves the pair label and corpus intact, but changes the loss contribution of individual tokens. The decision boundary is therefore inside the training record, not at the level of discarding an entire chosen-rejected pair.
