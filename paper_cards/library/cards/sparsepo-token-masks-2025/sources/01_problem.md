Response-level preference objectives normally apply reward pressure and KL regularization across all tokens in both completions. Yet many preferences are determined by a few words or phrases, such as a toxic expression, a sentiment-bearing word, or a factual statement. Uniform weighting can therefore force harmless context tokens to remain near the reference model and can blur the small positions that actually explain why one response is preferred.

The paper asks how preference optimization can decide, during training, which tokens should carry reward pressure and which should be held close to the reference. Its answer is to introduce masks directly into both token-level reward and KL terms. The masks may be supplied by reference-model activations or learned jointly with the policy, allowing the training objective to discover a sparse distribution of supervision instead of assuming all text is equally relevant.
