Vision-language preference optimization often assigns one reward to a whole chosen or rejected response. Hallucination, however, is usually localized: a few nouns, attributes, or relations may be unsupported by the image while surrounding text is harmless. Existing fine-grained approaches rely on expensive human segment labels, fixed hallucination rewards, or separate scoring models. They do not readily show which generated tokens are actually grounded in visual input at the current training step.

The paper asks how existing multimodal preference pairs can produce scalable token-level grounding rewards without new fine-grained annotations. It measures how each token's output logits change when the original image is replaced by a corrupted version. A large change marks a visually anchored token. The training objective then rewards or penalizes that token differently according to whether it occurs in the preferred or dispreferred response, updating the signal as the policy changes.
