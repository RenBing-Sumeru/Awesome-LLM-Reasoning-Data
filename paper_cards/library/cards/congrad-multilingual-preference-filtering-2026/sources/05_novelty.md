Many multilingual alignment methods either translate a dataset, change sampling schedules, or modify optimization gradients after a batch has been chosen. CONGRAD instead makes gradient conflict a property of individual preference records and applies the remedy before the records become training data. That separation matters because it permits a standard DPO update while changing the data mixture that the update sees.

The method also combines a cross-language consensus with an iterative self-rewarding data loop. Its low-rank EMA implementation is not just an efficiency add-on: without compressed per-language gradient histories, the proposed record-level test would be impractical at the reported model scale. The paper therefore contributes a selection criterion, an executable mechanism for maintaining it, and evidence that it favors cross-lingually transferable preference signal over merely high self-reward.
