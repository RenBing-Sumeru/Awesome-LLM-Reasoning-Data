Joint preference alignment across languages can improve weak languages through transfer, but it can also make language-specific objectives pull the shared model in incompatible directions. A translated or self-generated pair may be a valid preference example within its own language while still pushing parameters away from what benefits the other languages. Treating every pair as equally trainable therefore makes multilingual data volume a poor proxy for useful training signal.

CONGRAD asks how an iterative preference-learning loop can decide which multilingual pairs to keep when the cost of a record is not only label noise but also cross-language interference. The paper frames the decision at the level of training examples: select pairs that support a shared update direction and reject pairs likely to create conflicts. Its target is the data that reaches optimization, rather than a new multilingual benchmark or a new preference objective.
