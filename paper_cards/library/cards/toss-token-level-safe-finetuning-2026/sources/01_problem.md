Safety-aligned models are often fine-tuned on mixed instruction data to gain new task ability, but that process can weaken the refusal behavior learned during alignment. Whole-example filtering is a blunt remedy: a response may contain a short unsafe span together with useful explanations, or it may be removed entirely even though most of its supervision is harmless. Conversely, retaining the full response can still send gradients through the tokens most likely to undermine safety.

The paper asks whether selection can be moved from examples to target tokens. TOSS compares how a safety-degraded reference model and a utility-oriented reference model assign loss to each response token, then uses their difference to decide which targets should stop contributing to supervised fine-tuning. The aim is to retain as much useful custom data as possible while changing the exact training signal that reaches the model.
