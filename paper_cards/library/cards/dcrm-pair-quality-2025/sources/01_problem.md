Preference optimization is often described as learning from a chosen and a rejected response, yet the pair itself can contain two different kinds of contrast. A useful contrast concerns helpfulness, correctness, or another target preference; an unhelpful contrast can be length, style, wording, or probability differences that the training objective will nevertheless amplify. Selecting only the largest reward gap does not distinguish these two cases.

DCRM asks how to identify response pairs whose learnable preference signal is dense rather than diffuse. It studies chosen-rejected pairs used by offline preference optimization and turns pair quality into a measurable selection decision. The objective is not to replace the preference label or invent a new reward model, but to select records in which the desired difference is large relative to unrelated variation.
