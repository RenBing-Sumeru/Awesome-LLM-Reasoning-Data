DCRM assumes that a reward-model margin represents desired learning signal and that the two distance terms capture irrelevant variation well enough to normalize it. Both assumptions can fail when the reward model is biased, when useful improvements require substantial rewriting, or when the reference policy assigns unreliable likelihoods. Its Best-of-N-squared procedure also grows quadratically with the candidate pool and is therefore not a free filter at large scale. The evidence is based on the paper's conversational preference constructions and judge-based benchmarks, so a reuse should compare against simple reward-margin selection, audit the retained pairs manually, and measure both quality and diversity after training.
