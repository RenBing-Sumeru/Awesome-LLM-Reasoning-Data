The paper treats an SFT corpus as a model-relative input rather than a universally good or bad asset. Its central empirical rule is that lower perplexity of a training dataset under the base model predicts larger downstream improvements more consistently than embedding similarity to the evaluation benchmark or average sequence length. Perplexity is interpreted as a practical compatibility signal: data already linguistically and structurally accessible to the base model is easier to use productively in SFT.

A second idea connects the data decision to how the model changes. The authors compare layer-wise parameter deltas and find that changes in middle layers correlate more strongly with accuracy improvement than the largest changes near the top. Together, the released experiment grid makes it possible to inspect a complete path from source dataset and training configuration to evaluation behavior and model update, instead of inferring a rule from a few final checkpoints.
