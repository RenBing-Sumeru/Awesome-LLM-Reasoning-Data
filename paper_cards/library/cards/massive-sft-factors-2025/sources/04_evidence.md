The primary evidence is scale under a controlled grid: twelve base models, ten training datasets, full and LoRA tuning, and more than 1,000 resulting SFT models. Across the reported comparisons, Alpaca and UltraChat tend to give broad gains, MathInstruct and OpenMathInstruct help math tasks, and Magicoder helps code tasks, whereas FLAN is often harmful outside Natural Questions. The analysis finds that roughly five principal components explain more than 90% of variance in data-by-benchmark effects, while model family still changes which datasets are most helpful.

For the proposed compatibility signal, lower base-model perplexity has the clearest negative association with downstream gains and is stronger than semantic similarity or mean token length in the reported analysis. The layer study places the strongest correlation between weight change and accuracy at relative layer position about 0.6. A further 1k-versus-20k comparison finds no consistent accuracy advantage from 20,000 samples, and the latent-space analysis shows LoRA trajectories nearly overlap full tuning. These results support correlations and controlled trends; they do not establish perplexity as a causal quality label or a replacement for task-level evaluation.
