Direct Preference Optimization usually constructs one preferred and one dispreferred response for each prompt. For reasoning, a single correct-versus-incorrect pair can be a poor estimate of the true learning direction: answers can fail for different reasons, a sampled correct answer can be atypical, and one pair can place probability mass on misleading local patterns. Adding new external labels or rewriting the loss is often proposed to mitigate this uncertainty.

The paper asks whether the preference record itself can be made more reliable by observing multiple responses before training. It replaces a single pair with a preferred group and a dispreferred group, then compares their expected rewards. In the self-training setting, correctness partitions sampled rationales into the two groups, allowing a verifiable final answer to create richer preference supervision without requiring a separate human annotation for every rollout.
