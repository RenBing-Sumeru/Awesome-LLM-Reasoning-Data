Selecting a small task-specific fine-tuning set can save substantial training cost, but many selectors first fine-tune a scoring model on the same target collection. The selection pass can then cost as much as or more than training on the full dataset, while simple gradient or heuristic scores do not fully use the predictive behavior of the model that will eventually be fine-tuned.

The paper asks whether a pre-trained model can score training records without a separate target-data training phase. It uses each candidate record as a few-shot demonstration, measures how well the model answers held-out queries in that context, and treats the result as evidence of the record's training utility. Attention weighting is added because a demonstration's influence depends on its position and interaction with queries.
