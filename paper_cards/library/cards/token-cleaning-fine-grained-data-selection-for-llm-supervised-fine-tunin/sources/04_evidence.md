The paper compares full-token SFT, uniform random token retention, fixed cleaning, and self-evolving cleaning on LLaMA-3.2-3B. Its table uses a 0.6 selected-token proportion and reports that the cleaning methods consistently improve downstream performance over full-token and random-token baselines; the point is that the same example count can yield a different result when the loss mask changes.

The evidence also distinguishes two claimed regimes. The analysis gives an error-bound trade-off between lower token-label noise and fewer effective tokens, while the experiments show fixed cleaning is stable but limited and self-evolving cleaning can improve more. The latter is not uniformly safe: the paper explicitly analyzes a Matthew effect in which early weak reference quality can make hard groups worse.
