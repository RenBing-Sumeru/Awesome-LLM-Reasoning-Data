Prior work proposes individual rules for detecting bad preference pairs, but usually measures each rule inside its own experimental design. PrefCleanBench changes the contribution from another cleaning rule to a shared evaluation surface: the same kinds of records are cleaned, trained on, and evaluated under a common downstream protocol. It also makes the corrective action—removal versus label flipping—a first-class variable.

The novelty is thus comparative and operational rather than a new preference objective. The benchmark unifies thirteen methods across three families, four source datasets, several training algorithms, and multiple backbones, with modular released code. It shows that preprocessing decisions belong to the alignment-training pipeline and must be judged by their effect on the policy that consumes the cleaned records.
