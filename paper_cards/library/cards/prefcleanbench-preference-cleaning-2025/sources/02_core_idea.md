The paper's central move is to treat a cleaning method as a training-data intervention, not as a score that is good in isolation. It groups thirteen existing methods into LLM-as-a-judge, reward-model, and heuristic families, then makes each family produce a cleaned version of the same preference data. The comparison is anchored on what an aligned policy does after training with that version.

Each method has two decisions: how to identify an unreliable pair and whether to remove it or flip its label. PrefCleanBench keeps these decisions visible, varies data sources, base models, and optimization algorithms, and measures both alignment and generalizability. This separates a method that appears effective through a favorable setting from one that consistently improves the data consumed by training.
