Online preference learning repeatedly samples responses, labels a new chosen-rejected batch, and trains the next policy. Binary labels compress the reasons for a preference, so an iterative process can overproduce the dominant style, tone, safety, background-knowledge, or informativeness feature that its judge favors. This feedback loop reduces diversity and can degrade alignment dimensions that were initially present in the seed data.

PFP asks how new online preference records can be generated and consumed without drifting away from the original distribution of preference features. It treats those latent reasons as data-governance signals that should shape prompts, response generation, and later optimization. The contribution is an intervention in the online data cycle, not a new static preference dataset.
