Existing online preference methods generally generate new responses under a fixed prompt, label the pair, and optimize on it; distribution drift is an outcome rather than an explicit data-control variable. PFP changes that process by attaching inferred preference features to each seed record, mapping new instructions to feature targets, and constructing the online pair under synthesized prompts that preserve the target feature mix.

The contribution is not simply using conditioning prefixes or curriculum learning. Its reusable novelty is to make a distribution over reasons for preference constrain the generation and consumption of online data. Before reuse, verify feature-extraction agreement, inspect whether feature matching conflicts with a new safety policy, and test whether the preserved distribution represents users rather than an artifact of the seed annotators or the extractor model.
