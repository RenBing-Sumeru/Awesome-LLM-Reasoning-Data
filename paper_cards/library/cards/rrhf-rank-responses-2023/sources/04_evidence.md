The paper reports alignment performance comparable to PPO on Helpful and Harmless under reward-model scores and human labels. Its experiments also show that RRHF behaves like a best-of-n learner: stronger sampled candidates create a stronger possible training signal.

This evidence supports a simpler consumer for ranked data, not a guarantee that rankings are noise-free or transferable. A poor candidate pool can make a correct ranking uninformative, and a biased ranker can systematically teach the wrong response style.
