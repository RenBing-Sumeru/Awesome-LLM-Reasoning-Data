Prior scalable DPO pipelines often regard reward ranking as sufficient: highest is chosen and lowest is rejected. This work makes the rejected response a first-class design decision and tests it systematically over a prompt-local distribution. It changes data construction at the level that DPO actually sees, showing that a larger observed margin is not necessarily a better learning signal.

The contribution is not a new reward model or an alternate pairwise objective. It is a calibrated sampling policy that uses mean and variance to choose representative response positions, then identifies a reusable high-versus-moderately-low pair. The seven-point grid and 21 pair datasets make the claim falsifiable: the reported optimum must beat both max-min and nearby reward intervals under the same DPO recipe.
