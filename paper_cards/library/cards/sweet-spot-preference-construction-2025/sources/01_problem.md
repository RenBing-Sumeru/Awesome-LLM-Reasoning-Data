On-policy preference construction commonly samples several responses per prompt, chooses the highest reward as chosen and the lowest reward as rejected, and trains DPO on that pair. When sampling is scaled from a handful to hundreds of responses, this max-min rule can become worse rather than better: the minimum can be an outlier that provides an unhelpful learning contrast, while more samples increase the chance of such extremes.

This paper asks how the two records in a DPO pair should be selected from a prompt-specific reward distribution as the number of on-policy samples grows. It changes the preference data consumed by DPO, not the DPO loss. The target is a pair with a meaningful reward separation but a rejected answer that remains a useful negative, so expanding generation budget improves rather than destabilizes post-training.
