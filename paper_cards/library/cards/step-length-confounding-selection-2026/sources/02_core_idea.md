The authors call the bias step length confounding. Across four source models, selected examples under existing naturalness rules have longer steps than unselected examples, while long steps receive higher average log probability. Their explanation is structural: the first token of a step often opens a new reasoning branch and has low probability, so a long step gives that penalty less weight in a token average.

ASLEC changes how the selection score handles those first tokens. ASLEC-DROP omits them before averaging log probability. ASLEC-CASL instead fits a causal-style linear regression in which the ratio of first tokens is a confounder, then subtracts its estimated influence from the global score. Both variants still prefer high-adaptability trajectories, but attempt to prevent a response from winning merely because its steps are longer.
