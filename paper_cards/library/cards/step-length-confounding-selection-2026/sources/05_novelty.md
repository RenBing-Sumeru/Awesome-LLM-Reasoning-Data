Earlier naturalness filters treat a response's average token probability as a direct measure of model-data compatibility. This paper identifies a specific aggregation artifact in that assumption: a repeated low-probability position is normalized away more easily when each reasoning step contains many tokens. The contribution is therefore an audit of the score itself, followed by two corrections that leave the candidate trajectories and target SFT objective otherwise unchanged.

The new element is not selecting high-probability data in general, nor detecting that long answers can be biased. It is locating the bias at step starts and making the first-token ratio an explicit statistical object in selection. DROP tests the simplest removal intervention, while CASL retains first-token information but estimates its confounding contribution. The paired score correction and selected-data distribution analysis are essential because a higher final accuracy alone would not demonstrate that step length has been disentangled.
