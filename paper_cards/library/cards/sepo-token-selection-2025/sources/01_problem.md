Conventional preference optimization treats every token in a chosen or rejected response as equally useful. This can inject large amounts of weak or noisy supervision, although a small set of extreme tokens may account for most of the preference signal. Existing selective methods often depend on expensive fine-grained labels or complicated token-selection procedures, making them difficult to apply to ordinary pairwise preference data.

The paper asks how to identify the tokens that actually drive an existing response-level preference without collecting new token annotations. It proposes to train a modest DPO oracle on a sampled subset of the target data, use the oracle/reference probability ratio as a token-level reward estimate, and train the target policy only on the selected extremes. The decision is therefore not whether to keep a response pair, but which parts of that pair should affect the objective.
