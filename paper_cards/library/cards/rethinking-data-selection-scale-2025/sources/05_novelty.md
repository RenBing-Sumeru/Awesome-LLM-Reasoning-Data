Earlier selection papers often compare one proposed selector with one random draw, sometimes on a pool too small for selection variance to be visible. This work changes the evidential unit from “the score of one chosen subset” to a distribution of outcomes obtained under repeated random controls and significance testing at large scale. It also places simple token length and diversity heuristics in the same comparison, making it harder to mistake complexity for value.

Its novelty is therefore a scale-sensitive audit of how selection rules are justified, not a new selector, reward model, or optimizer. A nearby paper that offers a clever score without repeated-random stress tests answers a different question and is not evidence for this paper's conclusion. The contribution changes the acceptance criterion for a training-data decision: a selector should earn its use by beating the variance-aware baseline, not simply by producing a plausible ranking.
