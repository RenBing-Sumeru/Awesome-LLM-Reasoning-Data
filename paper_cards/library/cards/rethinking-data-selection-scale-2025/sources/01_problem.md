Many instruction-data selection methods are introduced on relatively small pools and evaluated against a single random subset. Their scores are then treated as evidence that a self-scoring rule will remain useful when the candidate pool reaches millions of examples. At that scale, however, a selection gain can be entangled with subset variance, model choice, source mixture, or an inexpensive proxy such as length, so one favorable draw is not enough to establish a reliable training decision.

This paper asks a narrower but consequential question: after repeating the sampling and fine-tuning experiment, which data-selection decisions still beat random selection at scale? It reproduces several self-scoring and diversity-oriented selectors on OpenHermes2.5 and filtered WildChat, treating the selected instruction-response subset as the intervention. The goal is not to invent another score, but to test whether existing scores justify the extra selection cost under controlled supervised fine-tuning.
