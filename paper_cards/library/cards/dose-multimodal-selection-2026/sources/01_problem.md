Visual instruction tuning is expensive because its image, instruction, and answer triples are numerous, uneven in value, and sometimes poorly aligned. Existing selectors often depend on gradients, loss traces, or a model trained on the very corpus it will screen. Those requirements make the selector costly to deploy on a fresh collection and can entangle its judgments with prior exposure to the data.

The paper asks whether fixed, off-the-shelf models can decide which multimodal records should reach the instruction-tuning consumer. Its answer is to treat textual suitability and image-text relevance as separate evidence, then choose records through a distribution-aware sampling rule. The practical target is a smaller training subset that does not merely retain the highest scores, but remains broad enough to support downstream generalization.
