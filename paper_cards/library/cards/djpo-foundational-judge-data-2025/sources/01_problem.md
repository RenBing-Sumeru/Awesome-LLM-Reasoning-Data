LLM judges are increasingly used to evaluate, filter, and supply feedback for model training, yet many are trained by supervised imitation on small, narrow evaluation datasets. Such a judge may learn to reproduce a correct label without learning why it is correct, may fail on a new evaluation format, and has little exposure to incorrect candidate judgments. These limitations weaken its value as a reusable source of reward or data curation.

The paper asks how to construct preference data for a broadly capable judge rather than only train a model to emit ratings. It samples multiple candidate evaluations and uses agreement with a ground-truth judgment to create positives and negatives. It then adds three complementary learning views: explain the judgment with a critique, state the judgment directly, and infer the judged response from a correct evaluation. The aim is to train one consumer that can reason about evaluations, not merely mimic labels.
