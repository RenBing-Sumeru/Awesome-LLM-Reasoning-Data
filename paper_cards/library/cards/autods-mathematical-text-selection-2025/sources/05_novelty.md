Earlier curation pipelines commonly depend on labeled preference data, heuristic source rules, embedding similarity, or a separately trained quality classifier. AutoDS changes the decision interface by asking a generative model a short binary question and reading its own next-token scores as a continuous selector. That choice keeps the scoring mechanism close to the model's generative representation while avoiding a new supervised classifier-training stage.

The novelty is therefore the publicly released scored corpus together with a reproducible, model-relative ranking rule. Continual pretraining, generic question prompting, and mathematical benchmark evaluation are not new on their own. The contribution matters when the research question is which records a training run consumes; it should not be read as a general verifier for mathematical answers or as evidence that every model will agree with Qwen-72B's ranking.
