Broad web corpora contain mathematical text with very different educational value. A page with notation, a solved derivation, a research abstract, and a fragment of malformed markup can all look “mathematical” to a keyword filter, yet they do not supply the same learning signal to a language model. Human labels can distinguish them, but collecting enough labels for web-scale continual pretraining is costly; training a separate classifier also introduces another model and annotation pipeline to maintain.

The paper therefore asks whether a large generative model can act as a zero-shot data selector without task-specific supervised labels. It turns the model's relative preference for YES and NO answers to mathematical-value questions into a continuous score, then uses that score to decide which documents from several public sources enter the AutoMathText mixture for continual pretraining. The problem is data consumption: how to allocate a fixed training-token budget to more useful mathematical text.
