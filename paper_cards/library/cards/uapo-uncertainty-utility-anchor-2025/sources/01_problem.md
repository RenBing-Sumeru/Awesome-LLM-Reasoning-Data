Many offline preference objectives assume that every training record is a clean winner-loser pair and that annotators act as fully rational utility maximizers. Real preference collections can be imbalanced, contain unpaired responses, and include uncertain or inconsistent labels. Converting all records into strict pairs requires additional ranking or reward-model work and can discard useful responses, while optimization on shifted data can amplify unreliable reward signals.

The paper asks how an offline objective can retain more of these imperfect records without treating every stated preference as equally certain. It introduces a prompt-conditioned utility anchor that represents a reference utility and an uncertainty-aware penalty around it. The intended change is both data-level and optimization-level: unpaired examples become admissible training material, while the anchor moderates the direction inferred from uncertain annotations.
