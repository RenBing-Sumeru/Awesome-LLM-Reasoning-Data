## TL;DR
HelpSteer 2 releases a CC-BY-4.0 reward-model training dataset of prompts, responses, and five human-annotated quality scores: helpfulness, correctness, coherence, complexity, and verbosity.

## 1. What is this work?
This is a NeurIPS 2024 Datasets and Benchmarks Track paper from NVIDIA. It documents HelpSteer 2, a public dataset used to train reward models for SteerLM-style alignment.

The paper should be read primarily as an open release of scalar, attribute-level human feedback data for reward modeling. It is not a reasoning benchmark and does not make the attribute scores a proof of factual correctness, safety, or reasoning validity.

## 2. What data object does it expose?
The exposed object is a per-response feedback record:

- a prompt,
- a candidate response,
- five scalar human ratings: helpfulness, correctness, coherence, complexity, and verbosity.

The prompt source is reported as more than 95% ShareGPT plus a small proprietary enterprise prompt subset. Responses are generated by internal Nemotron models, Mixtral-8x7B-Instruct-v0.1, and humans.

## 3. What is the verifier / reward / judge / environment?
The verifier is human judgment supplied through Scale AI by US-based annotators. The released supervision is answer-level scalar attribute feedback.

The feedback contract is `judgment_required`: scores are subjective and task-dependent, and should not be treated as executable checks or ground-truth verification.

## 4. How is the data constructed?
The paper describes a pipeline with:

- prompt collection from ShareGPT and a small proprietary enterprise subset,
- response generation from Nemotron-family models, Mixtral-8x7B-Instruct-v0.1, and humans,
- filtering of non-English and coding prompts,
- prompt clustering and selection using BERTopic and prompt-complexity signals,
- annotation of responses on five quality dimensions,
- extra annotation or post-processing for high-disagreement cases,
- reward-model training with NVIDIA NeMo-Aligner / SteerLM 2.0 tooling.

Unknown or not fully specified from the official public sources used here: rollout count, temperature, inference budget, full prompt distribution, and exact proprietary prompt composition.

## 5. How can it enter post-training?
The direct training use is reward modeling. The released data can train or evaluate reward models over response-level attribute scores.

It should not be described as process supervision, tool supervision, or a verified reasoning trace dataset. Any conversion to pairwise preferences, RL reward signals, or verifier targets requires an explicit downstream construction rule.

## 6. What should readers audit?
Before reuse, readers should audit:

- the exact Hugging Face dataset revision and split,
- how the five scalar attributes are aggregated or weighted,
- whether reward-model training normalizes or rebalances attributes,
- overlap with downstream evaluation prompts,
- the effect of excluding non-English and coding prompts,
- disagreement handling and any reannotation rule,
- license compatibility for ShareGPT-derived prompts and released annotations.

## 7. What is missing or risky?
Main risks:

- scalar scores can hide why a response was preferred,
- helpfulness and verbosity may become shortcuts for reward modeling,
- US-based annotation may encode a narrow preference distribution,
- ShareGPT lineage can introduce contamination or unknown prompt provenance,
- the proprietary prompt subset is only partially inspectable,
- filtered domains limit reuse for coding, multilingual, or specialized reasoning tasks.

These risks do not make the dataset unusable, but they are central to any audit-ready reuse.

## 8. Why it matters for post-training reasoning data
HelpSteer 2 is a compact, inspectable example of attribute-level reward feedback data. For this catalog, it helps separate scalar reward-model supervision from pairwise preference data and from process-level verification.

It is useful for the Preference & Reward Feedback Data track because it exposes both the feedback object and enough construction metadata to support artifact-level audit.
