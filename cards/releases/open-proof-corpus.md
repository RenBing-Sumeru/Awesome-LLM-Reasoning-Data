## TL;DR

The Open Proof Corpus releases thousands of natural-language competition-mathematics proofs generated by six frontier models and graded by qualified human judges. Its records can include positive or negative proof-validity labels, written feedback, uncertainty flags, optional sentence-level error annotations, solver identity, source metadata, and best-of-N selection information.

Its main value is not merely corpus size. It exposes a rare expert-feedback object for studying proof verifiers, process supervision, best-of-N selection, and human-label reliability. It must nevertheless be used with strict split controls: MathArena, PutnamBench, and test are evaluation-oriented subsets, not proof-training data.

## 1. What is this work?

- Year / venue: 2026 · ICLR 2026.
- Atlas roles: data release, benchmark, verifier/reward resource, process-supervision resource, and construction recipe.
- Domains: mathematical proofs, olympiad mathematics, high-school mathematics, and undergraduate mathematics.
- Current metadata status: partial.
- Source material: official materials from more than 20 national and international mathematics competitions, plus selected MathArena and PutnamBench subsets.
- Core contribution: frontier-model proofs paired with expert correctness judgments, written grading feedback, optional localized error annotations, provenance fields, and split-specific evaluation protocols.
- Why it belongs: it makes both successful and failed proof attempts available as feedback-bearing records instead of reducing proof generation to final-answer accuracy alone.

The release should be understood as a human-judged natural-language proof corpus. It is not a formally checked theorem-proving corpus, and its binary labels do not provide executable correctness guarantees.

## 2. What data object does it expose?

The primary record is a mathematical problem paired with a generated natural-language proof and human evaluation.

Recorded or optionally recorded fields include:

- problem identifier and statement;
- generated proof;
- solver model identifier;
- one or two human correctness labels;
- written grading feedback;
- judge uncertainty flag;
- optional sentence-level error annotations;
- one or two judge identifiers;
- competition, level, source, source URL, and year;
- optional official solution and issue notes;
- optional model thinking;
- token counts, generation cost, and timestamp;
- O4-MINI proof summary and suggested issues;
- best-of-N selector metadata.

The current public Hugging Face snapshot is described in the metadata as containing 4,934 rows across six splits:

| Split | Public rows | Intended reading |
|---|---:|---|
| `generic` | 3,039 | General released proof records; training eligibility still depends on provenance and task policy. |
| `test` | 292 | Held-out evaluation; must not enter training. |
| `putnambench` | 564 | Benchmark analysis; must not enter training. |
| `matharena` | 310 | Benchmark analysis; must not enter training. |
| `best_of_n` | 252 | Selector and best-of-N analysis with nonuniform candidate-label coverage. |
| `pass_at_n` | 477 | Repeated-sampling analysis rather than an automatically safe training split. |

The paper reports 5,062 proofs, whereas the checked public snapshot contains 4,934 rows. SMT withholding is documented, but the complete 128-row difference is not yet reconciled.

## 3. What is the verifier / reward / judge / environment?

- Native verifier: qualified human mathematical-proof judges.
- Judge pool: thirteen expert human judges are recorded in the accepted metadata.
- Input: a problem, generated proof, and, when available, an official competition solution or other reference material.
- Primary output: a binary judgment of whether the complete proof is mathematically correct under the corpus grading guidelines.
- Additional outputs: written feedback, uncertainty flags, and optional sentence-level annotations identifying erroneous passages.
- Supervision granularity: answer level, step or sentence level when annotations exist, and scalar/binary reward.
- Terminal predicate: a human judge accepts or rejects the complete proof as mathematically correct.
- Quality-control mechanism: a subset receives duplicate grading; approximately 10% double grading means that most proofs retain only one human judgment.
- Reported agreement: 90.4% agreement is recorded, but nonzero disagreement remains.
- Estimated label error: the paper's reported 5% judge-error estimate depends on independence assumptions and should not be treated as a directly observed universal error rate.
- Auxiliary model assistance: O4-MINI supplies non-verdict proof summaries and issue suggestions. These are not the authoritative correctness labels, but they may still anchor human review.
- Best-of-N contract: eight O4-MINI generations are all human-graded for 60 problems. For other problems, only selected outputs may be graded, so unselected candidates must not be silently interpreted as human-rejected proofs.

The feedback contract is therefore expert judgment over natural-language proof validity. It is richer than final-answer matching but weaker than formal proof checking.

## 4. How is the data constructed?

1. Problems are collected from official materials for more than 20 mathematics competitions, with additional MathArena and PutnamBench benchmark subsets.
2. Some items may pass through optional GPT-4.1 translation followed by manual verification.
3. Six frontier models generate proofs: O4-MINI, O3, Gemini-2.5-Pro, Grok-3-Mini, Qwen3-235B-A22B, and DeepSeek-R1.
4. Most problems receive proofs from about five models rather than from one shared generator.
5. Generation uses model-default parameters with a maximum generation length of 64,000 tokens.
6. Exact temperature and aggregate rollout count are undisclosed in the accepted metadata and remain `unknown`.
7. Judges grade proofs through a custom expert interface, assign binary correctness labels and written feedback, and may add uncertainty or sentence-level annotations.
8. Malformed problems or solutions flagged by judges are removed.
9. MathArena proofs are retained only when their final answer is correct.
10. Records are assigned to training-capable, benchmark, test, best-of-N, or pass-at-N subsets.
11. Best-of-N uses repeated O4-MINI generations, but exhaustive eight-candidate human grading is limited to 60 problems.
12. The public corpus is exported through the official Hugging Face dataset.

The release also describes OPC-R1-8B, initialized from the paper's 8B DeepSeek-R1-Qwen3-family model. Its verification training adapts `verl` and GRPO with train/validation model selection. That experiment supports a narrow verifier-training and RL use claim; it does not turn the corpus labels into formally verifiable rewards.

## 5. How can it enter post-training?

Recorded uses include reward modeling, process supervision, verifier-oriented GRPO/RL training, evaluation, and audit.

Appropriate uses include:

- train a proof-validity reward model from positive and negative proofs;
- train a verifier to predict expert binary judgments;
- use written feedback or localized error annotations for process supervision;
- study false-positive and false-negative behavior on nearly correct proofs;
- compare best-of-N selectors against exhaustively graded candidate groups;
- audit solver-model failure patterns across competitions and difficulty levels;
- evaluate whether a verifier transfers across proof generators and source competitions.

Important restrictions:

- exclude `matharena`, `putnambench`, and `test` from training;
- do not treat every unselected best-of-N candidate as a negative label;
- do not collapse written feedback and sentence annotations into a uniform field without checking missingness;
- do not describe human acceptance as formal proof verification;
- preserve solver, source, split, judge-count, and selection metadata when deriving training examples.

## 6. What should readers audit?

- Is the exact Hugging Face revision pinned before analysis or training?
- Does the selected subset exclude MathArena, PutnamBench, and test?
- Are `best_of_n` records separated into fully graded eight-sample groups and selector-only graded groups?
- Does each example have one judge or two, and are disagreements preserved?
- Are adjudication outcomes available for conflicting labels?
- Are uncertainty flags and sentence annotations missing at random, or concentrated on difficult proofs?
- Are O4-MINI summaries visible to judges, and could they anchor the verdict or error localization?
- Are positive and negative rates compared separately for each solver model and competition?
- Are source URLs official, public, and still accessible?
- Are competition-problem and official-solution redistribution rights known at item level?
- Are translations distinguishable from original-language problem statements?
- Is overlap with model pretraining, benchmark corpora, or other public proof datasets measured?
- Is the 5,062-paper versus 4,934-public-row difference reconciled?
- Are generation parameters, code revision, dataset revision, and model identifiers pinned?
- Does downstream evaluation separate verifier quality from generator quality and best-of-N budget?

## 7. What is missing or risky?

- Most proofs have only one human judgment because duplicate grading covers approximately 10% of the corpus.
- Reported 90.4% agreement still leaves meaningful label noise on subtle or nearly correct proofs.
- The reported 5% judge-error estimate depends on assumptions about judge independence.
- Fewer than 3% of proofs are marked uncertain, but difficult proof defects may remain underreported.
- O4-MINI issue summaries may anchor judges even though the authors report no significant agreement shift.
- Best-of-N supervision is selective: exhaustive grading of all eight candidates covers only 60 problems.
- Some best-of-N records use a V1 ranking judge whose method is described by the official dataset card as suboptimal.
- The paper/public-release row-count discrepancy is unresolved beyond documented SMT withholding.
- Some source URLs are private or missing.
- Dataset-level CC BY-NC-SA 4.0 does not resolve item-level rights for every competition problem or official solution.
- Broader contamination against generator pretraining data and other mathematical corpora is `unknown`.
- Exact generation temperature and aggregate rollout count are `unknown`.
- Natural-language proof labels cannot replace Lean, Isabelle, Coq, or another executable proof checker.
- Reuse without split and lineage fields could convert benchmark proofs into hidden training data.

## 8. Why it matters for post-training reasoning data

Open Proof Corpus exposes an unusually complete feedback-bearing record: source problem, generated proof, solver identity, human verdict, written explanation, optional local error spans, judge metadata, generation cost, and selection context.

This supports questions that answer-only math datasets cannot address:

- What kinds of invalid proofs pass superficial final-answer checks?
- How consistent are expert judges on near-correct reasoning?
- Do verifiers transfer across generator families?
- Does best-of-N selection improve genuine proof validity or merely exploit judge preferences?
- Which local proof errors are recoverable through process supervision?
- How much do single-judge labels limit reward-model calibration?

Its strongest contribution is therefore not a claim that expert judgment is infallible. It is the release of positive and negative natural-language proofs with enough feedback and provenance to study where human proof verification works, where it disagrees, and how those judgments can safely enter post-training.
