## 1. Problem: What question does this work address?

Process verifiers face a cost-quality trade-off. Discriminative PRMs can assign a quick binary label to each step but often struggle with incomplete traces and cannot explain subtle errors. Generative reward models can reason about a step and produce a diagnosis, but generating a long critique for every correct step is expensive. Both approaches also depend on process labels that are costly to annotate manually and noisy when estimated from rollouts.

Dyve asks whether a verifier can learn when a one-token confirmation is sufficient and when a step requires a longer explanation. The associated data problem is more important than the architecture alone: how can noisy MCTS-derived error hypotheses be filtered and transformed into supervision that teaches this adaptive choice?

## 2. Core idea: What is the main contribution?

Dyve learns two output modes through supervised fine-tuning:

- **System 1:** emit a single `+` token for a correct, straightforward step.
- **System 2:** emit a detailed analysis in `<think>...</think>` followed by `+` or `-` for a complex or erroneous step.

The mode is not selected by a hand-coded threshold. It is learned from a curated mixture of short and long targets. The construction pipeline starts with OmegaPRM MCTS traces, checks proposed error steps against a full-trace DeepSeek-V3 judgment, rebalances positive and negative examples, and asks DeepSeek-R1-Distill-Qwen-32B to produce detailed System-2 targets. DeepSeek-R1-Distill-Qwen-14B is then fine-tuned as the released verifier.

The native feedback signal is step-level correctness with optional explanation. During inference, Dyve verifies the reasoning prefix sequentially and halts at the first parsed error.

## 3. Method: How does it work?

### Initial trace pool

The authors collect approximately 15,000 mathematical questions, primarily from GSM8K and MATH. OmegaPRM applies divide-and-conquer MCTS with UCB-style state selection and generates about 20 rollouts per question. The pool is augmented with MathShepherd and RLHFlow process-reward data. PRM800k is excluded to reduce leakage into ProcessBench evaluation. This stage yields roughly 1.2 million traces with step-by-step reasoning and potentially noisy error-step hypotheses.

### Consensus filtering and rebalancing

DeepSeek-V3 receives each full rollout and checks whether the OmegaPRM-identified error step is credible. A rollout is kept when the judge confirms the error assessment and discarded when the two disagree. The paper reports that this removes approximately half of the noisy rollouts. Retained examples are then rebalanced to obtain a desired mixture of correct and incorrect steps, although the target ratio and sampling policy are not quantified.

The paper describes the resulting curated set as approximately 117K reasoning traces. The current official Hugging Face dataset exposes 156,321 training rows, so the paper count and live release are not the same version or unit. The release page does not explain the difference.

### System-1/System-2 target synthesis

For a completely correct, straightforward trace, each step is paired with a one-token `+` verification. For a divergent trace, preceding correct steps receive System-1 targets; at the first flagged error, DeepSeek-R1-Distill-Qwen-32B generates a detailed explanation followed by `-`, and the training sequence stops. This produces a mixed target distribution whose output length encodes the desired verification mode.

### Training and inference

DeepSeek-R1-Distill-Qwen-14B is fine-tuned with standard next-token cross-entropy on the curated targets. Ablations also use Llama-3.1-8B-Instruct, Qwen2.5-14B, and Qwen3-8B. At inference, Dyve conditions on the problem and cumulative reasoning prefix, generates between one and up to 8192 tokens, parses the final verdict, and halts when an error is detected.

For Best-of-N evaluation, Dyve scores solutions proposed by DeepSeek-R1-Distill-Qwen-14B or Qwen2.5-MATH-7B-Instruct on MATH-500 with `N` in 1, 2, 4, and 8.

## 4. Evidence: Why should we believe it?

On ProcessBench, the paper compares Dyve with discriminative PRMs, general LLM judges, and reasoning-model judges across GSM8K, MATH, OlympiadBench, and OmniMATH subsets. Ablations show three distinct effects: a stronger reasoning base improves verifier performance; DeepSeek-V3 consensus filtering improves over unfiltered Monte Carlo labels; and DeepSeek-R1-32B stepwise flagging adds further gains by creating explicit short and long targets.

The paper reports that consensus filtering raises MATH F1 from 34.7 to 56.0 in the shown ablation, while final stepwise flagging reaches the full Dyve result. Base-model ablations show that the same mixed supervision improves Qwen2.5-14B, Qwen3-8B, and DeepSeek-R1-14B, although the DeepSeek base performs best on harder mathematical subsets.

Efficiency evidence reports the fraction of System-1 responses and average verification time by ProcessBench subset. A System-2-only model is slightly more accurate in the reported table but substantially slower; this supports Dyve's intended accuracy-efficiency compromise rather than claiming that dynamic verification is always the most accurate option.

Best-of-N experiments show improvements over majority voting and a System-1-only verifier for two proposer models. These results establish downstream selection utility on MATH-500, not the correctness of every process label.

The code, a live dataset, and a Dyve-14B checkpoint are public. The model card declares MIT. However, the repository and dataset page do not display explicit licenses, and the live dataset count is not reconciled with the paper.

## 5. Novelty: What is new relative to prior work?

The main novelty is not merely mixing classifier and generative verifier outputs. Dyve encodes the choice between them in the target data and learns the selection policy through ordinary SFT. Output length and explanation behavior emerge from the supervision mixture rather than a manually tuned confidence threshold.

The construction pipeline also moves consensus filtering from a trace-level cleanup step into a staged process-supervision recipe: MCTS proposes error locations, a separate judge audits them in full context, a stronger reasoning teacher writes first-error explanations, and the final verifier learns both cheap and expensive responses.

This makes verification budget a data-design decision. Which steps receive only `+` and which receive long explanations determines the learned allocation policy at inference time.

## 6. Limitations: What are the weaknesses or hidden assumptions?

- DeepSeek-V3 agreement is treated as a quality filter without reporting retained-label precision, recall, calibration, or human agreement. Consensus can preserve correlated errors rather than remove them.
- OmegaPRM, DeepSeek-V3, DeepSeek-R1-32B, and the DeepSeek-R1-14B student share mathematical and model-family priors. Error hypotheses, acceptance decisions, explanations, and the verifier can therefore reinforce the same blind spots.
- The paper says approximately 117K curated traces, while the official HF release has 156,321 rows. Neither a version tag nor a schema-level explanation resolves whether these are traces, step examples, or later additions.
- The paper reports that about half the rollouts are discarded but does not release rejected traces, judge disagreements, filter logs, or confusion analyses. The most informative failure data is therefore unavailable for audit.
- Rebalancing changes the natural prevalence of correct and incorrect steps, yet the target distribution, sampling weights, and effect on calibration are not reported.
- The dataset page and GitHub repository do not display a license. The model card declares MIT, but that does not license the training data, code, imported PRM records, or generated outputs.
- GSM8K, MATH, MathShepherd, and RLHFlow have separate provenance and licensing. The paper states that public data are used under their licenses but provides no row-level inherited-license manifest.
- PRM800k exclusion addresses one leakage path, not semantic overlap with ProcessBench, base-model pretraining, or imported sources.
- Exact MCTS budgets, `c_puct`, rollout generator configuration, temperatures, seeds, teacher prompts, and immutable model revisions are incomplete.
- A System-2 explanation is generated after a teacher flags an error. It can be a plausible post-hoc rationale rather than a faithful account of why the step is invalid.
- First-error truncation provides no labels for downstream steps and assumes that later reasoning is not independently useful after divergence.
- Evaluation is mathematics-centric. The adaptive policy may not transfer to code, science, agents, or open-ended reasoning.
- The output parser, special tokens, and maximum 8192-token analysis create brittleness and cost exposure. Malformed or excessively long explanations can complicate reward use.

For deployment, calibration must be measured separately for one-token and explanatory modes. False positives allow flawed steps to pass; false negatives prematurely halt valid traces. A mode ratio or average F1 is not sufficient because the cost of each error differs by use case.

## 7. Uses: How can this work be used?

Dyve is a reusable process verifier for Best-of-N selection, search pruning, stepwise error detection, and process-reward supervision. Its mixed outputs support low-latency acceptance for simple steps and human-readable diagnoses when a trace appears complex or wrong.

For Track 8, the strongest contribution is the data refresh pattern. A curator can generate process candidates, preserve initial error hypotheses, obtain an independent full-trace judgment, retain agreement and disagreement records, synthesize explanations only for selected cases, and retrain the verifier. Unlike the current release, an audit-ready implementation should preserve both accepted and rejected examples, version every teacher and filter, and publish calibration by source and mode.

The recipe can also guide selective-compute verifiers outside mathematics, but only after rebuilding the teacher, filter, and error taxonomy for the target domain. Reusing the checkpoint alone does not demonstrate transfer.

## 8. Reading notes: What should readers remember?

- Dyve learns the fast/slow choice from target format; it does not apply a hand-coded confidence threshold.
- The native signal is step-level `+`/`-`, with a long explanation only for selected complex or incorrect steps.
- The pipeline is OmegaPRM proposal → DeepSeek-V3 agreement filter → rebalancing → DeepSeek-R1-32B explanations → DeepSeek-R1-14B SFT.
- About 1.2M noisy traces become approximately 117K in the paper, but the live HF release contains 156,321 rows.
- Consensus-filtered does not mean independently verified; rejected disagreements and calibration data are missing.
- The model is MIT-declared, while dataset and code licensing remain unclear on their official pages.
