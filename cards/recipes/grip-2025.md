## 1. Problem: What question does this work address?

Synthetic reasoning datasets often scale by rewriting seed questions or conditioning generation on externally supplied topics. These approaches can stay close to their seeds, inherit narrow coverage, and become costly when they depend on proprietary teachers. Filtering a large noisy pool is another option, but it transfers cost and uncertainty to post-processing.

GRIP asks whether a small seed set can first be abstracted into a reusable structure and then recombined to synthesize many more questions. The concrete target is mathematical instruction tuning: turn the 7.5K MATH training questions into a substantially larger corpus while maintaining meaningful concept combinations, complete problems, and plausible solutions using open-weight models.

## 2. Core idea: What is the main contribution?

GRIP separates concept discovery from question generation. It extracts key concepts from the MATH seeds, consolidates them into approximately 10K concepts, and constructs a Key Concepts Relationship Graph. Graph paths and communities define four combination types—one-hop, two-hop, three-hop, and community—that become conditions for synthesizing new questions without copying the seed question into the generation prompt.

The reported output is GRIP-MATH, 2.1M mathematical question-solution pairs. Questions are generated from concept combinations, solutions are routed to different Qwen2.5-Math models by predicted difficulty, and three open mathematical models filter both problem and solution quality. This 2.1M figure is a paper claim, not a presently verified downloadable dataset.

## 3. Method: How does it work?

### Knowledge-base construction

Qwen2.5-32B extracts no more than five key concepts from each of the 7.5K MATH training problems. Qwen2.5-7B removes concepts that are vague, mathematically wrong, or excessively detailed. An embedding model then groups likely synonyms: similarities from 0.90 to 1.0 are grouped directly, similarities from 0.70 to 0.90 receive an additional LLM judgment, and lower similarities are treated as distinct. The LLM selects or synthesizes a representative label for each group, leaving about 10K concepts.

### Relationship graph and synthesis

Concepts that co-occur in a seed problem receive explicit edges. Indirect graph paths supply implicit relationships. GRIP constructs questions from:

- one-hop pairs that already co-occur in the seed set;
- two-hop pairs connected by a shared neighbor;
- three-hop pairs between a hub concept and a more distant concept, after low-weight combinations are removed;
- fully connected communities of three or four concepts.

Qwen2.5-32B synthesizes a new problem from the selected concepts without seeing the original seed question. A separate, unnamed rating model assigns difficulty. Qwen2.5-Math-7B generates solutions for low- and medium-difficulty questions, and Qwen2.5-Math-72B handles high-difficulty questions.

### Multi-model filtering

DeepSeek-R1-Distill-Qwen-7B, Qwen2.5-Math-Instruct-7B, and DeepSeek-Math-RL judge every synthesized item. For questions, each model scores logical completeness and presentation quality from 0 to 1; the scores are weighted by the models' reported mathematical ability, and records below 0.85 are removed. For solutions, every judge produces a binary decision, and any negative vote rejects the pair.

The paper reports that eight A100 GPUs running for 36 hours produced and scored approximately 2.1M final pairs. Downstream evaluation fine-tunes several 1.5B–7B models with LLaMAFactory, but the exact end-to-end synthesis scripts and data manifest are not publicly verified.

## 4. Evidence: Why should we believe it?

The paper trains Qwen1.5-7B, Mistral-7B, Llama-3-8B, Llama-3.1-8B, Qwen2-1.5B, and Qwen2-7B on GRIP-MATH and evaluates mathematics, science, general reasoning, and code-oriented benchmarks. Reported gains across multiple base models support the claim that the generated pairs contain useful training signal; they do not establish that every generated derivation is correct.

The construction analysis compares expansion ratio, estimated synthesis cost, embedding similarity to seeds, and novelty of concept combinations with prior synthetic math datasets. GRIP reports a 280-fold expansion and 71.8% of concept combinations not appearing in the seed set. A 500-item human annotation study compares the three-model judging ensemble with GPT-4.1, and an ablation reports improved question scores after key-concept filtering.

The method is described beyond the abstract: prompts, teacher assignments, graph definitions, concept thresholds, model filters, compute estimates, and downstream training settings appear in the paper and appendices. However, the evidence is not accompanied by an accessible code repository or GRIP-MATH artifact that would let readers reproduce these numbers or inspect individual lineage records.

## 5. Novelty: What is new relative to prior work?

GRIP's main distinction is treating a seed dataset as a concept co-occurrence graph rather than as a collection of rewrite targets. Explicit graph edges preserve familiar combinations, while indirect paths deliberately generate combinations absent from individual seed questions. This separates the semantic substrate used for generation from the wording of the source problem.

The pipeline also replaces a single proprietary quality judge with three open mathematical models and makes the question threshold and unanimous solution rule explicit. Its novelty is therefore the combination of graph-derived synthesis conditions, difficulty-routed solution generation, and open-model selection—not a new formal correctness verifier.

## 6. Limitations: What are the weaknesses or hidden assumptions?

The largest limitation is artifact availability. The NeurIPS checklist says code will be released, but no official code repository, GRIP-MATH download, project page, schema, immutable revision, model checkpoint, or release license was verified during curation. The paper's 2.1M result cannot currently be inspected as a release.

Graph proximity is only a heuristic for conceptual compatibility. Indirectly connected concepts can produce contrived, underspecified, or unsolvable questions. Concept extraction and clustering can also merge distinct mathematical ideas or preserve synonyms, and the exact effect cannot be audited without intermediate graph artifacts.

The three judging models are correlated open mathematical models. Shared misconceptions, answer-style preferences, or benchmark exposure can survive unanimous voting. Their natural-language judgments do not formally prove that each derivation is valid, and the paper does not publish false-positive or false-negative rates for the final 2.1M pool.

The release contract is incomplete: raw candidate counts, stage-specific rejection counts, rejected questions, failed solutions, decoding parameters, the difficulty-rating model, record-level seed lineage, and train-validation splits are undisclosed or unavailable. No systematic overlap check is reported against the mathematical evaluation sets.

Finally, the MATH seed license, generated-question license, generated-solution license, and model-output redistribution terms are not established by a verified release artifact. This blocks confident reuse even if a copy of the data is found elsewhere.

## 7. Uses: How can this work be used?

As a Track 8 recipe, GRIP provides a concrete blueprint for building reasoning data from a compact seed corpus: extract concepts, consolidate a vocabulary, construct an explicit/implicit relationship graph, generate by relationship type, route solution teachers by difficulty, and apply separate question and solution gates.

Researchers can use the design to compare seed rewriting with graph recombination, measure how graph distance affects validity and novelty, or replace model-only voting with symbolic checking and human audits. The explicit thresholds also make GRIP a useful case for studying judge correlation, selective filtering, and how reported scale changes after independent verification.

GRIP-MATH itself should not be treated as a reusable open release until an official dataset, license, schema, revision, and provenance manifest are available. For now, the work is best used as a documented construction design and an audit checklist for future releases.

## 8. Reading notes: What should readers remember?

- GRIP starts from 7.5K MATH training problems and reports 2.1M synthetic question-solution pairs.
- Its central object is a roughly 10K-node concept relationship graph, not a seed-question rewrite prompt.
- Questions need a weighted score of at least 0.85; solutions require unanimous approval by three open mathematical models.
- Multi-model agreement is a selection heuristic, not formal mathematical verification.
- No official code, dataset, schema, license, rejected pool, or immutable version was verified.
- Treat GRIP as a promising recipe with incomplete release evidence, not as an audit-ready dataset.
