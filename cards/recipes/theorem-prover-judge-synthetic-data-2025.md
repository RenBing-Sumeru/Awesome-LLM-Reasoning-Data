## 1. Problem: What question does this work address?

Synthetic mathematical reasoning is often filtered by final-answer matching or an LLM judge. A correct final answer can hide invalid intermediate steps, while model judges can introduce correlated bias. Formal theorem provers offer executable guarantees about a formal statement, but converting natural-language questions and solutions into Lean is itself error-prone. Discarding every failed formalisation also selects for short, simple, prover-friendly examples.

The paper asks how theorem-prover feedback can be integrated into synthetic-data generation without treating initial autoformalisation failure as mathematical failure. It also asks how Verified and False outcomes can become supervised and preference-learning data rather than a single accept/reject stream.

## 2. Core idea: What is the main contribution?

Theorem Prover as a Judge, or TP-as-a-Judge, inserts Lean verification between synthetic generation and post-training. Questions are generated by reverse question answering, formalised, translated back into natural language for an alignment check, and then answered by two LLMs. Each solution is formalised step by step and assigned one of three outcomes:

- **Verified:** every formalised step executes and is accepted by Lean.
- **False:** at least one formalised step is refuted.
- **Error:** Lean cannot execute the formalisation, so the result is unresolved rather than mathematically false.

Execution errors trigger iterative autoformalisation with Lean error messages for up to five retries. The final pair of answer outcomes determines the training action: Verified/Verified becomes SFT data, Verified/False becomes a DPO preference pair, and False/False is disposed. RLTPF then trains a model with one SFT epoch followed by three DPO epochs.

## 3. Method: How does it work?

### Synthetic question and answer generation

The pipeline uses few-shot reverse question answering over GSM8K and MATH solutions to generate new mathematical questions. The principal domains are problem solving, algebra, and counting and probability. GPT-4o and `chatgpt-4o-latest` generate two step-by-step answers for each retained question. Generation uses temperature 0.0 and a 3,500-token maximum; questions are few-shot and answers are zero-shot.

### Question formalisation and alignment

Question filtering has four phases:

1. CoMAT converts the natural-language question into a symbolic representation.
2. GPT-4o produces a Lean formalisation and executes it.
3. GPT-4o auto-informalises the formal statement back into natural language.
4. A second GPT-4o check compares the original and reconstructed questions.

Questions that fail this round-trip alignment are excluded before answer formalisation. The formalisation prompt uses 10-shot examples for GSM8K, 8-shot for MATH algebra, and 6-shot for MATH counting and probability.

### Step verification and iterative autoformalisation

Each natural-language answer is converted into a sequence of formal proof steps. Lean checks every step. A refuted step produces False; a syntax, tactic, theorem, or execution problem produces Error. For Error, the current proof and Lean diagnostic are returned to GPT-4o for refinement, with a maximum of five retries. The paper reports that this raises formalisation execution from 60% to 87%, with most successful corrections occurring by the third iteration.

### Training-data routing

For the two generated answers:

| Outcome pair | Action |
|---|---|
| Verified / Verified | Use verified responses for SFT |
| Verified / False | Form a preferred/rejected pair for DPO |
| False / False | Dispose |
| Error | Retry autoformalisation, then remain unresolved if retries fail |

The SIMQA corpus contains 3,508 response records: 1,876 derived from GSM8K and 1,632 from MATH. The training allocation is 2,562 SFT examples plus 473 DPO pairs, representing 946 paired responses.

RLTPF applies LoRA to Llama-2-7B-chat, Llama-3.2-3B-Instruct, Llama-3.1-8B-Instruct, OLMo-2-1124-7B-Instruct, and Mistral-7B-Instruct-v0.3. Training uses one SFT epoch and three DPO epochs with effective batch size 64, RMSprop, learning rate `3e-5`, seed 42, and DPO beta 0.1.

## 4. Evidence: Why should we believe it?

The paper evaluates RLTPF-trained models on SVAMP, GSM8K, MultiArith, a 500-example GSMSymbolic subset, MATH-500, AQUA, AIME 2024, and OlympiadBench. It compares direct prompting, chain-of-thought prompting, model training on SIMQA, and larger math-specialized systems. The reported results show gains from the 3,508-example training set across several base models.

An allocation ablation on Llama-3.1-8B compares SFT on all, only rejected, or only verified instances, and then SFT plus RLTPF. SFT on verified examples followed by DPO performs best in the displayed table. SFT on all instances followed by DPO performs worse, supporting the paper's claim that treating rejected traces as ordinary imitation targets conflicts with later preference learning.

A 2,000-example construction analysis reports that about 30% fail during question formalisation. Additional categories separate answer-formalisation errors, verified solutions, and false solutions. Iteration analysis shows that longer sequences tend to require more retries and that some examples still fail after repeated attempts.

The evidence supports the utility of theorem-prover-based routing on the reported mathematical domains. It does not demonstrate that the Lean formal statement is always semantically faithful to the original question, that SIMQA is contamination-free, or that the released artifacts reproduce the reported results.

The paper explicitly links an official code repository, but `https://github.com/joshuaongg21/RLTPF` returned 404 on 2026-07-11. No accessible official SIMQA dataset, formal-proof release, checkpoint, or Hugging Face collection was confirmed. The ACL paper remains available under CC BY 4.0.

## 5. Novelty: What is new relative to prior work?

The main contribution is a three-way distinction between mathematical refutation and formalisation failure. An execution Error is not automatically rejected; it enters a bounded correction loop driven by Lean diagnostics. This reduces the simple-example bias created by one-shot formalisation filters.

The work also turns theorem-prover outcomes into an explicit data-allocation contract rather than a binary quality score. Verified traces support imitation, verified/false contrasts support preference learning, and jointly false pairs are removed. This connects formal process verification directly to SFT and DPO corpus design.

Finally, question formalisation is audited through auto-informalisation and alignment checking before answer verification. This recognizes that executing the wrong formal statement is not useful, even if Lean accepts it.

## 6. Limitations: What are the weaknesses or hidden assumptions?

- Lean verifies the generated formal statement, not the original natural-language semantics. A proof can be executable and internally correct while formalising the wrong question, assumption, or reasoning step.
- GPT-4o performs formalisation, auto-informalisation, and alignment checking. Reusing one model family creates correlated self-validation risk rather than an independent semantic audit.
- Execution Error and mathematical False are correctly separated, but the final 87% execution rate still leaves unresolved cases. Excluding them can bias the corpus toward concepts and phrasings that GPT-4o and the selected Lean environment formalise easily.
- Retry count correlates with sequence length. A five-retry cap makes complexity part of the selection rule and can preferentially remove long proofs.
- The paper focuses on problem solving, algebra, and counting/probability. Formal verification outside mathematics, and even across broader mathematics, remains open.
- Problem difficulty is bounded by the generators' ability to produce and solve their own reverse-generated questions.
- Verified/Verified examples enter SFT without preserving disagreement diversity. False/False pairs are disposed, discarding potentially valuable negative traces and verifier-failure evidence.
- A Verified/False pair assumes the formalisation is equally faithful on both sides. Formalisation quality can confound which response becomes preferred.
- Round-trip alignment is a judgment contract with undisclosed calibration. The paper does not report independent human accuracy for question equivalence or step-faithfulness.
- Exact GPT snapshots, Lean version, theorem library, prompts, source-row identifiers, retry histories, and disposed records are not consolidated in an immutable manifest.
- Decontamination against evaluation benchmarks and base-model pretraining is unknown.
- The paper reports approximate dataset-generation cost, but retries make per-sample cost variable and formal verification remains compute intensive.
- The official repository is currently unavailable. Code license, SIMQA license, data schema, rejected traces, formal proofs, logs, and trained checkpoints cannot be audited or reproduced from the cited release.

The formal verifier has low tolerance for syntactic and library mismatch, creating false negatives through Error. Its more serious false-positive mode is semantic misalignment: an accepted proof of a statement that does not faithfully represent the intended question or solution. Mitigation requires independent alignment annotation, versioned Lean environments, preserved formalisation attempts, and audits that sample both accepted and rejected cases.

## 7. Uses: How can this work be used?

TP-as-a-Judge provides a template for constructing mathematical reasoning data with executable intermediate-step feedback. Dataset builders can use the routing table to separate demonstration data, preference pairs, retry queues, and hard rejections instead of collapsing all outcomes into one pass/fail field.

For Track 8, iterative autoformalisation is a useful release recipe because it treats failed verification as a recoverable construction state. An audit-ready implementation should preserve the natural-language trace, every formal proof attempt, Lean diagnostics, retry count, final outcome, alignment judgment, source row, model revision, and training allocation.

The framework can also support process-verifier benchmarks, formal reward models, and curriculum design based on formalisation difficulty. Its current empirical evidence does not justify reuse outside mathematics without a domain-appropriate formal substrate and new alignment checks.

## 8. Reading notes: What should readers remember?

- TP-as-a-Judge has three verifier outcomes: Verified, False, and execution Error.
- Error triggers up to five Lean-guided repair attempts; it is not treated as mathematical incorrectness.
- Training allocation is explicit: Verified/Verified → SFT, Verified/False → DPO, False/False → dispose.
- SIMQA has 2,562 SFT examples and 473 DPO pairs, totaling 3,508 response records.
- Formal execution is only as meaningful as the natural-language-to-Lean alignment.
- The paper's official GitHub link currently returns 404, so data, code, checkpoints, licenses, and retry provenance are not reproducible from the cited artifact.
