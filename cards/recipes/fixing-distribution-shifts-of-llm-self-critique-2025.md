## 1. Problem: What question does this work address?

Self-critique systems are often trained on critiques and corrections generated by a teacher or an earlier model, then deployed on reasoning produced by the updated policy. That mismatch creates distribution shift: the critic learns to diagnose one generator's mistakes but must operate on another generator's current errors. SCOP asks how to keep reasoning, critique, and correction supervision aligned with the policy being optimized while still deriving rewards from answer-verifiable mathematical tasks.

## 2. Core idea: What is the main contribution?

SCOP maintains an on-policy self-play buffer whose complete episode contains a question, current-policy reasoning, a critique of that reasoning, multiple critique-conditioned corrections, extracted final answers, and stage-specific rewards. Instead of assigning critique quality from a static teacher label, it estimates whether a critique is useful by Monte Carlo sampling corrections and measuring how often those corrections solve the problem. The refreshed buffer then supports PPO updates of the same policy.

The method is not teacher-free. Qwen2.5-72B-Instruct creates the initialization critiques and corrections used before on-policy learning. The contribution is the transition from that teacher-initialized starting point to current-policy trajectory refresh, not elimination of external supervision from the entire pipeline.

## 3. Method: How does it work?

SCOP has two main phases.

### Teacher-initialized pair SFT

The initialization stage uses GSM8K and MATH examples. DeepSeek-Math-7B-Base and Qwen2.5-7B-Base are the reported base-model families, while Qwen2.5-72B-Instruct supplies critiques and corrections. Initialization items are retained only when at least six of eight sampled annotator corrections are correct. The paper reports approximately 60,000 initialization examples, but no corresponding rows or manifest are released.

Pair-SFT teaches the model the coupled reasoning-to-critique and critique-to-correction behavior needed before reinforcement learning. This phase establishes the format and initial capability of the self-play policy; it also imports the teacher's error taxonomy, critique style, and answer-extraction assumptions.

### On-policy self-play and PPO

The RL prompt pool is built from cleaned Numina-CoT. Prompts that the current policy always solves or that the annotator never solves across eight samples are removed. For each retained prompt, the current policy generates the reasoning, critiques, and corrections that populate the on-policy buffer.

The reported sampling design draws four critiques and eight corrections per critique, producing 32 correction rollouts per reasoning episode. Sampling uses temperature `0.6`, top-p `0.9`, and a maximum output length of 2,048 tokens.

Final-answer verification supplies programmatic stage outcomes. A correction receives reward based on whether its extracted answer matches the gold answer. Critique reward is estimated by the success rate of sampled corrections conditioned on that critique, making it a finite Monte Carlo estimate of downstream usefulness. A hallucination penalty is applied when the critique attacks reasoning that is already correct. The resulting reasoning-correctness, critique, hallucination flag, corrections, and critique/correction rewards are stored as one feedback-bearing episode.

The current policy is then updated with PPO using the stage rewards and a reported KL penalty of `0.05`. New trajectories are sampled from the updated policy, refreshing the buffer and reducing the stale-teacher distribution mismatch.

## 4. Evidence: Why should we believe it?

The ACL paper evaluates the approach on mathematical reasoning and self-correction settings using the reported DeepSeek-Math and Qwen2.5 base models. Its comparisons and ablations examine the value of on-policy training, critique rewards, and different components of the self-play recipe. These results support the paper's central claim that current-policy critique/correction training can outperform static-data alternatives under the tested setup.

The method description discloses key sampling and optimization settings: four critiques, eight corrections per critique, temperature `0.6`, top-p `0.9`, 2,048 output tokens, Pair-SFT, PPO, and KL coefficient `0.05`. However, no public trajectories, reward records, initialization data, prompt manifest, checkpoints, or executable implementation were present in the verified official repository. The evidence is therefore paper-level rather than artifact-reproducible.

## 5. Novelty: What is new relative to prior work?

SCOP makes critique-data freshness an explicit training variable. Rather than training a critic/refiner once on teacher-generated errors, it regenerates the entire reasoning-critique-correction episode from the current policy before policy updates. It also evaluates a critique indirectly through the success distribution of multiple conditioned corrections, turning downstream repairability into a process reward.

## 6. Limitations: What are the weaknesses or hidden assumptions?

- The initialization remains dependent on Qwen2.5-72B-Instruct. Teacher style, failure modes, and licensing constraints can shape the policy before self-play begins.
- Critique reward is a finite Monte Carlo estimate from eight corrections. It is noisy and entangles critique quality with correction sampling variance and the current model's ability to follow feedback.
- A critique that identifies a real error may score poorly if the correction policy cannot repair it; a vague critique may score well if the model already tends to produce the correct answer.
- The hallucination rule is coarse. Final-answer correctness does not prove that every intermediate step is sound, and answer extraction can mis-handle equivalent forms.
- Four-by-eight sampling is expensive and makes the effective buffer depend strongly on temperature, top-p, output limit, model revision, and random seeds.
- Removing prompts that are always solved or never solved concentrates training on a middle-difficulty band and may discard both mastered skills and hard capabilities worth learning.
- PPO with model-generated rewards can collapse or exploit answer-extraction artifacts despite KL regularization.
- Decontamination is unknown. The exact cleaning applied to Numina-CoT and overlap with evaluation tasks is not documented in the metadata.
- The official repository currently contains only an MIT license and `.gitignore`; it has no implementation, configs, data, trajectories, manifests, or checkpoints.
- The repository license does not establish terms for unreleased teacher outputs, generated trajectories, prompt sources, or future checkpoints.

## 7. Uses: How can this work be used?

SCOP is a recipe for building a current-policy buffer that preserves the full reasoning-to-critique-to-correction lineage and assigns separate rewards to critique and repair stages. It can inform RLVR systems that need critique refresh, experiments comparing off-policy teacher data with on-policy self-play, and audits of whether critique rewards measure diagnosis or merely correction-policy competence. A faithful implementation should retain question source, policy checkpoint, reasoning, answer extraction, critique, hallucination decision, every correction rollout, reward calculation, sampling knobs, PPO revision, and buffer round.

## 8. Reading notes: What should readers remember?

- “On-policy” applies to the refreshed RL buffer; initialization still uses a 72B teacher.
- The data object is a complete reasoning-critique-correction episode, not an isolated critique.
- Critique reward is estimated from eight sampled repairs and inherits their variance.
- Prompt filtering defines which difficulty band enters self-play.
- Final-answer matching is the terminal contract, not native proof of process correctness.
- Treat the empty official repository as a blocking reproduction gap, not as a completed code release.
