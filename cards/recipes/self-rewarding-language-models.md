## TL;DR

Self-Rewarding Language Models uses a model's own LLM-as-a-judge feedback to create preference data for iterative DPO.

The data object is self-generated feedback. It should be audited for judge-policy shared blind spots rather than treated as independent human preference data.

## 1. What is this work?

The paper studies a loop where a model generates candidate responses, judges them, converts those judgments into preference pairs, and trains with DPO.

For this atlas, it is a recipe card for RLAIF and synthetic preference feedback.

## 2. What data object does it expose?

The relevant record can include:

- prompt,
- candidate response,
- self judgment,
- score,
- chosen response,
- rejected response,
- iteration.

Seed data includes OpenAssistant-derived instruction and evaluation fine-tuning examples, plus augmented prompts generated by Llama 2-Chat 70B.

## 3. What is the verifier / reward / judge / environment?

The verifier is model-judged. The policy model acts as an LLM-as-a-judge and produces scores or preferences over its own candidate outputs.

The terminal predicate is whether the self-judge prefers one model-generated response over another.

## 4. How is the data constructed?

Reported construction details include:

- seed with instruction fine-tuning and evaluation fine-tuning examples,
- filter EFT examples where generated score ranking matches human OpenAssistant rankings,
- generate augmented prompts with Self-Instruct-style prompting,
- sample candidate responses,
- produce self-judgments and chosen/rejected pairs,
- iterate DPO over self-generated preference data.

Reported counts include 3,964 AIFT pairs for one DPO iteration and 6,942 pairs for the next.

## 5. How can it enter post-training?

Recorded use:

- preference_learning.

Use it as a synthetic preference recipe. Do not describe the self-judgments as human preference labels or independent reward-model supervision.

## 6. What should readers audit?

- Self-confirming reward loops can amplify model bias.
- The judge and policy share blind spots.
- Self-judgment can reward style instead of substance.
- Prompt-generation artifacts may shape what the model learns to prefer.
- Benchmark gains are not proof that self-generated feedback is clean.

## 7. What is missing or risky?

- Exact seed data and OpenAssistant license constraints.
- Whether generated prompts or preference pairs are released.
- Sampling settings for candidate responses and judging.
- Filtering rules for malformed or low-quality judgments.
- Whether the judge prompt matches the intended local preference standard.

## 8. Why it matters for post-training reasoning data

It is a canonical example of preference data generated by the same model family being optimized, which makes provenance and feedback independence central audit questions.
